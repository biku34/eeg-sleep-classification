1 · Executive summary

The system classifies every 30-second window of a single forehead EEG channel into one of five sleep stages — Wake, N1, N2, N3, REM — and does it at an agreement level comparable to human sleep technicians.

Three models were built in sequence, each beating the last. A transparent spectral Random Forest set the reference; a 1-D CNN learned features directly from the raw signal; and a CNN + BiLSTM added temporal context — the way human scoring rules actually work — to reach the headline result. All evaluation is strictly subject-independent (no person appears in both training and test), so these numbers reflect generalisation to new people, not memorisation.

Headline

κ = 0.700 is "substantial agreement" in sleep medicine and approaches human inter-scorer reliability (~0.76) — achieved from a single frontal electrode, the configuration a consumer wearable actually has. A causal, real-time demo reconstructs an unseen subject's whole night at 85% accuracy, and the model holds up on a completely different database (CAP) at κ 0.630 with no retraining — evidence it learned real sleep physiology, not dataset quirks.

The N1 story

N1 F1 climbed 0.296 → 0.422 → 0.474 across the ladder — a 60% relative gain. N1 is where single-channel staging is hardest (it looks like REM on EEG alone), so improving it is the clearest signal the models learned real structure. REM likewise jumped to 0.766.

6 · Confusion matrix — CNN + BiLSTM

Rows = true stage, columns = model's guess. A perfect model is 1.00 down the diagonal (darker green = higher). Off-diagonal reds are the mistakes.

What it shows: Wake, N2, N3 and REM all sit at 0.77–0.84 correct. N1 (0.55) remains the weakest and leaks mostly to N2 (0.23) and REM (0.11) — the expected, physiologically-explained failure: on one EEG channel, N1 and REM both look like theta activity, and the eye/muscle channels that would separate them are deliberately excluded (a wearable doesn't have them).

7 · Biomarkers — what the model keyed on

The Random Forest's feature importances are human-readable evidence that the model recovered real sleep physiology, not artefacts.

Physiology, rediscovered

Sigma (12–16 Hz, the sleep-spindle band that defines N2) and delta (0.5–4 Hz, deep-sleep slow waves) both rank among the drivers, alongside amplitude (std) and signal speed (zcr/beta). The model was never told these matter — it found them in the data.

9 · Leakage check — why these numbers are honest

The single most common way to fake a great sleep-staging score is to split data by epoch instead of by person: adjacent 30-second windows are near-identical, so random splitting lets the model "see the answer." This experiment proves the honest protocol was used.

Split methodAccuracyCohen's κ
Random epoch split (leaky, wrong)0.7130.609
Subject split (honest, used here)0.6840.568
Note

Even on a subsample (which weakens the effect), random splitting inflated accuracy by +0.029 and κ by +0.041. Every headline number in this report uses the honest subject-independent split — so they are real generalisation, not leakage.

10 · Cross-database robustness — the CAP test

The hardest generalisation test: take the model trained entirely on Sleep-EDF and run it, without any retraining, on recordings from the CAP Sleep Database — different acquisition hardware (512 Hz vs 100 Hz), a different electrode montage (F4-C4 vs Fpz-Cz), and different subjects. This measures whether the model learned real sleep structure or just quirks of one dataset.

Evaluated on 3,138 epochs from three unseen subjects. A κ of 0.630 remains "substantial agreement" — the model degrades gracefully, not catastrophically, which is the mark of features that generalise.

Where the drop comes from

Every error has a physiological reason

N2 stays rock-solid (0.88) — the most transferable stage. The degradation concentrates exactly where the montage change predicts: Wake→N1 (0.35) and REM→N1 (0.29), because CAP's F4-C4 derivation carries the alpha (wake) and theta (REM) rhythms differently than the Fpz-Cz the model trained on, so both drift toward N1. Some N3→N2 (0.30) appears too, as slow-wave amplitude reads lower on the bipolar montage. None of this is random — it is exactly the montage sensitivity the test is designed to expose.

11 · What each metric means

MetricPlain English
AccuracyFraction of 30-s windows labelled correctly. Intuitive but misleading under class imbalance.
Macro-F1F1 averaged over the 5 stages, each weighted equally — so ignoring rare N1 tanks the score.
Cohen's κAgreement with the expert, corrected for chance. The standard sleep-medicine metric; humans agree at ~0.76.
Subject-independent (LOSO/grouped)No person appears in both training and test — the rule that makes the score trustworthy.
